\title{Information Richness Ablation for Multimodal Land Cover Composition Regression: A Leakage-Free Study\\
{\large DI 725 Phase 2 Report}}

\author{\IEEEauthorblockN{\"Omer Faruk Merey}
\IEEEauthorblockA{Graduate School of Informatics, Middle East Technical University \\
omer.merey@metu.edu.tr}}

\maketitle

\begin{abstract}
We benchmark a CLIP cross-attention model for land-cover composition
regression on ARAS400k under strict no-leakage conditions, evaluating six
text-input levels across three seeds: empty caption, no-text architectural
floor, noun keywords, two qualitative captions, and a random-token control.
Removing cross-attention raises MSE by $\sim$36\,\% relative, while clean
captions help only when they correctly describe the dominant ground-truth
class --- a fidelity-conditional benefit that averages near zero on the
unstratified test set. The empty-caption baseline is significantly better
than random tokens ($p=8.6\!\times\!10^{-9}$), so caption \emph{content}
matters in addition to fusion capacity, but fidelity gates the effect.
\end{abstract}

\begin{IEEEkeywords}
remote sensing, land cover composition, multimodal learning, CLIP,
cross-attention, data leakage, ablation
\end{IEEEkeywords}

%-------------------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Methodology}
\label{sec:method}

\textbf{Research question.} \emph{How does the semantic richness of
auxiliary text captions --- ranging from a vision-only baseline, to simple
keywords, to detailed qualitative descriptions --- impact land-cover
composition regression while strictly avoiding data leakage?}

The dataset's \texttt{hybrid\_*} and \texttt{text\_qwen3-4b} captions
embed the regression targets verbatim, so training against them solves the
task lexically rather than multimodally. Phase 2 uses only the leakage-free
\texttt{vision\_*} captions; numbers are masked to avoid residual leakage.
Six conditions span the Information Richness axis
(Table~\ref{tab:conditions}), each trained with three seeds (18 runs).
A caption-fidelity stratified evaluation tags each test tile
\texttt{agree} or \texttt{disagree} by whether a synonym of the
argmax-class appears in the conditioning caption. R1 keywords are noun
lemmas extracted by spaCy without filtering to the seven target classes,
since hand-built class mappings would inject GT structure into the input
and constitute soft leakage.

\begin{tabular}{@{}lll@{}}
\toprule

\midrule
R0a    & CLIP+CA+MLP  & empty token \\
R0b    & CLIP+GAP+MLP & --- (no text path) \\
R1     & CLIP+CA+MLP  & noun-lemma NER over both \texttt{vision\_*} \\
R2a    & CLIP+CA+MLP  & sanitized \texttt{vision\_gemma3-4b} \\

\bottomrule
\end{tabular}

Phase 1 was a single-sample forward-pass proof-of-concept; Phase 2 is
the first end-to-end training of the architecture. CLIP ViT-B/32 encoders
are frozen to isolate the impact of the fusion layer and prevent
overfitting; only the 8-head cross-attention block and a 512-256-7 softmax
MLP head are trained. Loss is MSE on softmax outputs against GT/100.
Optimisation: AdamW (lr~$10^{-4}$, weight decay $10^{-4}$), cosine
annealing over 30 epochs, batch size 128, early stopping patience 5; data
is split deterministically 70/15/15. Because CLIP is frozen, image and
text embeddings are precomputed once as fp16 tensors and reused across
runs, reducing the 18-run grid from an estimated $\sim$10\,h online to
under 50\,min on an Apple~M4 MacBook~Pro (MPS backend). Phase 1
anticipated migrating to a CUDA GPU for full training, but the precompute
optimisation eliminated the need: only the cross-attention block and the
MLP head receive gradients, and the entire grid fits comfortably in M4
unified memory at batch size 128.

The architectural gap (R0b vs.\ R0a) is large and significant
($p\!\ll\!10^{-30}$). Paired $t$-tests on per-sample MSE further give
R0a $-$ R-rand $=-0.00081$ ($p=8.6\!\times\!10^{-9}$),
R2b $-$ R-rand $=-0.00049$ ($p=0.011$),
R2a $-$ R-rand $=-0.00036$ ($p=0.067$, marginal), and
R1 $-$ R-rand $=-0.00028$ ($p=0.13$, n.s.).
Among caption-bearing conditions, R2a and R2b sit between R-rand and R0a:
R2b clears significance over R-rand while R1 does not.

Stratifying by caption fidelity (Table~\ref{tab:fidelity}) reveals that
R2a/R2b match or beat R0a on tiles whose caption mentions the dominant
GT class, and underperform R0a by a comparable margin on tiles whose
caption does not. The two effects approximately cancel on the
unstratified test set. Per class, R0a is the lowest-error condition on
every land-cover type except Shrub, and R0b's largest gap is on the
\emph{Water} class ($3.5\times$ R0a's error), suggesting that fusion
capacity helps most on rare or visually distinctive classes.

\section{Discussion}

The headline gap on this dataset is architectural: cross-attention with
even an empty caption substantially outperforms a no-text pure-vision
floor. Without R0b in the design, this gain would have been credited to
multimodal fusion. The corollary R-rand result --- the empty caption is
significantly better than random tokens --- shows that caption content is
not irrelevant either; it is, however, dominated by fidelity.

Sanitised vision-only captions help when they describe the image
correctly and hurt when they do not, with the two effects of similar
magnitude. ``Adding captions'' is therefore not a uniform intervention,
and on this dataset the average effect on a fixed test set hides a
fidelity-conditional benefit that conditional routing could exploit. As
a concrete anchor: tile \texttt{0073.png} is 92\,\% Grass, yet both
\texttt{vision\_*} models describe a ``rugged, arid landscape'' with
``rocky terrain'' --- a clean caption that is qualitatively wrong, of
the kind that drives R2's losses on \texttt{disagree} tiles. The
fidelity-conditional pattern is also indirect support for the leakage
hypothesis: hybrid captions, which contain the GT percentages by
construction, would inevitably appear to ``help'' on average by an
amount far exceeding the small effect we observe with clean captions.

Inspecting R2a's cross-attention weights on test tiles shows
concentration on visually salient regions consistent with caption topics
(vegetated patches, channel boundaries) rather than tight per-token
following, consistent with the modest, conditional benefit observed.

Limitations: encoders frozen, single \texttt{synth} domain, single fusion
layer; the fidelity tag is a coarse lexical proxy.

%-------------------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Conclusion}

Cross-attention fusion contributes most of the apparent multimodal gain
on ARAS400k under leakage-free conditions; sanitised captions deliver a
small, fidelity-gated benefit that averages near zero unstratified.
Multimodal architectures should be ablated against architecture-matched
no-text and random-text baselines, and against fidelity-stratified test
splits, before any non-trivial multimodal gain is claimed.
